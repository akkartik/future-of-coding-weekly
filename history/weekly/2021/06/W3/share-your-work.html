Konrad Hinsen 2021-06-16 10:42:28

I really like the "accountability" framing of this post, and its analysis of various approaches in terms of trust and trust chains.

One aspect I find missing is that trust depends on everyone's personal context, and in particular on personal knowledge and competence, plus available resources. That's particularly true for proofs, which generate trust only for people who understand them. For everyone else, including many domain experts who have other things to do with their life, it's back to trusting experts and other authorities.

Another aspect of trust that is missing is a legal framework (liability etc.). It's historically very weak for software, making it low-risk to do harm to others, knowingly or accidentally.

An aspect that you hint at but don't develop in detail is collective rather than individual trust, i.e. reputation.

For mature technologies, all sources of trust come together into what is more a web of trust than a trust chain. I use my Bosch power drill with little hesitation because (1) I trust the brand because of its reputation, (2) it's a widely used tool resulting from a long history of development, so its potential risks are well known, (3) consumer protection laws are pretty strong for such equipment.
