Kartik Agaram 2020-10-09 20:54:52

I don't think your example of Nazi Germany shows what you think it does. Before they came to power and built up a propaganda machine, the Nazis gained influence by a combination of disinformation and physical violence that othered Jewish people. So free speech can in fact be used for othering.

I used to think Germany was being silly and fighting the last war by still banning anyone from articulating Nazi belief systems in public, down to the present day. But then I saw the effect that Radio Rwanda had on the genocide there, and that gave me pause. Germany has been closer to the abyss than the rest of us. We should assume they know something we don't.

The internet has given people new capabilities, and that is always accompanied by social turmoil as we figure out how to change the rules to find a new equilibrium. But clinging to "free speech" is just as reactionary as the side you're opposing. It's been shown up to be an ill-posed idea. Large parts of the phase space it encompasses are still valuable, but we need a new framework for pinning down the boundaries of the desirable space.

My current best-guess framing for the world we find ourselves in is to carve out exceptions to free speech when they incite violence. But there's still a lot of leaks to plug in that seam of the world.
